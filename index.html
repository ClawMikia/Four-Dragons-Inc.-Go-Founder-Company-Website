Volume II — Corporate Constitution

This should almost never change

  1. Every person deserves dignity.

  2. Good human beings come before great talent.

    Character outweighs skill. Skills can be learned. Character must be lived.

  3. Zero corruption.

    No bribery. No abuse of authority. No favoritism. No exploitation.

  4. The company exists to improve people's lives.

    Profit exists to sustain the mission — not replace it.

  5. Everyone is a creator.

    Teams may create. Individuals may create. Employees may pitch completely original projects. Passion projects are encouraged.

  6. No layoffs caused solely by financial optimization.

    Whenever possible: relocate people, retrain people, create new opportunities. Layoffs are considered only as a last resort if the company's survival is genuinely at stake.

  7. Anyone may resign peacefully to pursue their own dream.

    The company celebrates entrepreneurship. Leaving to build your own business is a success — not betrayal. Former employees remain part of the Four Dragons family.

  8. Treat everyone equally.

    Titles do not determine worth. Executives and interns deserve equal human respect.

  9. No forced participation.

    “Walang sapilitan.”

    Respect personal boundaries. Real teamwork comes from willingness — not coercion.

  10. Continuous learning.

    Everyone remains a student. Everyone remains a teacher.

Four Dragons Inc. Go · Atatakasa Aikido (温かさ) — Tokyo, Japan

Founder & Leadership

Christopher Lee Cajes serves as CEO and Chairman.

Geographic & Historical Landmark

Headquartered in Tokyo, Japan, this represents a monumental milestone — the first-ever pioneer initiative where a Filipino leader serves as Chairman and CEO of a domestic Japanese enterprise operating directly in the birthplace of Aikido. This presence echoes the path of Steven Seagal, the first Westerner to successfully manage an official martial arts organization and dojo infrastructure on Japanese soil.

The “Atatakasa” (温かさ) Philosophy

The organization takes its name from Atatakasa, meaning “Warmth” or “Kindness.” It translates O'Sensei Morihei Ueshiba's spiritual vision of universal harmony into a workspace and training hall defined by deep empathy, psychological safety, and unconditional care.

Corporate & Media Focus

A highly progressive, multi-industry corporate empire spanning video game development, immersive entertainment show production, and alternative Aikido leadership.

Radical Inclusivity & Open Sensei Policy

Structured as a tight-knit family empire where the Chairman works alongside his children, the company enforces absolute, uncompromised structural equality. Traditional martial-arts hierarchies, ranks, and dan boundaries are completely removed — visiting aikidokas, employees, and children train or work as equals regardless of rank or background. The headquarters operates an open-door policy: any martial artist ranked higher than the Chairman is enthusiastically welcomed to take the floor as guest sensei for the day or weekend. The organization maintains a standing, open aspiration to host Doshu Moriteru Ueshiba, should he ever visit, as leading master sensei.

The “Weekend Ukemi” Healing Protocol

A direct practical application of Atatakasa for healing life trauma within the family and company. When his children or students face immense emotional hardship, the Chairman intentionally steps down from the dominant nage (thrower) role on weekends and chooses to act strictly as ukemi — the receiver who takes the fall — for his students and higher-ranked practitioners. By reversing the traditional sensei power dynamic to absorb the physical momentum and impact, he creates a space of profound humility, mutual trust, and active emotional recovery.

Volume XI — Future Research

This book continuously evolves — artificial intelligence, global law, finance, psychology, education, entertainment, business, game design, animation, leadership, Japanese management philosophy, health, family wellness, and martial arts, among others.

One Recommendation

Most of this philosophy centers on compassion, freedom, and trust, which can make for a distinctive company culture. At the same time, every organization benefits from some shared expectations and coordination. Rather than eliminating all project-management practices, they can be adapted to fit these values — for example, replacing blame-oriented reviews with supportive check-ins, using lightweight planning instead of rigid processes, and encouraging feedback that helps people improve rather than assigning fault.

That approach stays true to the core idea:

“People come first. Great entertainment follows.”

It also creates a culture that is compassionate without losing the structure needed to build ambitious games, anime, films, and other creative projects over many years.
